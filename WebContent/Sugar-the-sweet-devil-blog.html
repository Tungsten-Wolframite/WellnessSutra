Sugar: the sweet devil


Regulate and control your sugar intake
Published in Mint-Leading Business Newspaper
Mon, Mar 02 2015. 06 25 PM

The effects of excessive consumption of sugar go well beyond calories

One-quarter of what you eat keeps you alive. The other three-quarters keeps your doctor alive,” says the hieroglyph on an ancient Egyptian tomb. I was reminded of this wisdom when I came across a paper in the British Medical Journal on how sugar consumption can be toxic. The paper by James DiNicolantonio, published in December, argues that excess sugar, not salt, is responsible for high blood pressure. Since heart disease often ensues as a result of high blood pressure, the paper argues that we should control our sugar intake if we want to keep hypertension and heart disease at bay.

Aashish Contractor, head of the department of rehabilitation and sports medicine at the Sir HN Reliance Foundation Hospital, Mumbai, is not convinced: “We need to take these results with a pinch of salt and I am not using this pun intentionally.” He says researchers sometimes try to negate the importance of one lifestyle modification when touting the benefits of another, to emphasize their point. It dramatizes the research findings, but it doesn’t necessarily give people an accurate picture. “I would say that both sugar and salt are important and I tell my patients to control the intake of both to keep blood pressure and heart disease in check,” says Dr Contractor.

Given that the process of making sugar from sugar cane was developed in India and is mentioned in the Atharva Veda, the consumption of sugar-laden foods is woven into our sociocultural fabric. No celebratory occasion is complete without a sweet offering and rationing sugar intake is tough. But ration it we must.

Multiple risks

Sugar is often considered a source of “empty” calories, but this couldn’t be farther from the truth.

A growing body of medical research shows us that the effects of excessive consumption of sugar go well beyond calories. Sugar can make us ill. Sugar in the form of high-fructose corn syrup (HFCS) is known to cause hypertension by increasing uric acid in the blood. HFCSs can be found in all kinds of processed foods and beverages, from cookies to soft drinks and ketchup. Sugar also increases the levels of triglycerides, a risk factor for heart disease, and causes insulin resistance, a risk factor for diabetes. It accelerates the ageing process by damaging proteins and DNA. Some early studies have linked sugar consumption to cancer.

An article published in the journal Nature in February 2012, titled The Toxic Truth About Sugar, by Robert Lustig, professor of clinical paediatrics at the University of California, US, and colleagues, argues that sugar poses so much danger to public health that its distribution and availability should be controlled like alcohol. Prof. Lustig argues that its effects are as deadly as alcohol’s and proposes adding taxes to processed foods that contain any form of added sugar, like fizzy drinks, juices and sugared cereals.

Anoop Misra, chairman, Fortis C-DOC Centre of Excellence for Diabetes, Metabolic Diseases and Endocrinology, New Delhi, agrees with the idea of a soda tax. He says there is enough research to show that taxing sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) by 20% could lead to a 3% reduction in obesity. In other words, taxing SSBs would reduce the consumption of sugar and lead to a reduction in the number of people becoming clinically obese. Last year, the excise duty on SSBs was increased by 5%.

The World Health Organization (WHO) recommends that people should get less than 10% of their caloric needs from sugar. In a draft guideline report last year, WHO opened this value for a public consultation to further reduce the amount to 5%. Here in India, the National Dietary Guidelines Consensus Group of experts recommended in a 2011 paper in the Diabetes, Technology And Therapeutics journal, that less than 10% of caloric needs should come from sugar. Dr Misra believes that like the WHO draft guidelines, this number needs to be reduced to 5% too, taking cognizance of the epidemic of hypertension and heart disease in our country.

Choose fresh food over processed items. So how much would 5% be in a 2,000-calorie diet? About seven teaspoons of sugar. This can be two cups of tea with a teaspoon of sugar in them, and a chocolate bar in an otherwise clean diet that has zero processed food and no sugar added to cooked food.

How can we reduce the amount of this sweet devil, in our diet? You probably know what the doctor would say—eat fresh fruit instead of desserts, avoid processed foods, reduce the amount of sugar in coffee and tea, and replace SSBs with coconut water, or unsweetened lemon or mint water.

But what do you do when a sugar craving hits? Cravings are caused by a drop in blood sugar levels, so aim to eat three main meals, with two healthy snacks in between.

The old adage, “Out of sight, out of mind,” does help—it’s difficult to snack on sweets if they are not there! Also, drink plenty of water; sometimes that can help subdue a craving. Finally, since deficiency in micronutrients can often masquerade as fatigue leading to sugar cravings, consider taking a good multivitamin supplement and fish oil capsules daily. All supplements need to be taken under a physician’s guidance.

If these tips don’t help, be prepared to explore the emotional issues around your sugar addiction with a wellness coach or a cognitive behavioural therapist.

Sujata Kelkar Shetty, PhD, is a wellness consultant and a clinical scientist trained at the National Institutes of Health in Bethesda, US.
