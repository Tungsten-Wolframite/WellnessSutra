Ayurveda | Ancient system, new faith


A growing body of scientific research is exploring the benefits of Ayurveda
Published in Mint-Leading Business Newspaper
Mon, Jun 17 2013. 07 47 PM

Seek Ayurveda from a reputed Ayurvedic physician while keeping its limitations in mind

Kala Shekhar, a 48-year-old homemaker in Bangalore, started getting searing pains in her left hip in 2009 and found herself limping to compensate. Shekhar took the painkillers prescribed by her allopathic physician and found that while she would get relief for 7-8 hours, the pain would return. She was told that she needed hip replacement surgery. She chose to change her course of treatment and went to the Institute of Ayurveda and Integrative Medicine (I-AIM), Bangalore, for Ayurvedic therapy—this reduced the pain. In May 2012, the pain returned with such severity that she couldn’t get out of bed. She returned to I-AIM, where she took a sustained course of treatment; she is now free of pain and can walk without support.

“Ayurveda is a wonderful medical system but people need to have patience, faith and the willingness to drink bitter-tasting medicines,” Shekhar says. She claims that there were no side effects from the medication and she no longer needs surgery.

G.G. Gangadharan, an Ayurvedic physician and medical director at I-AIM, defines Ayurveda as a holistic science and a healing tradition that has evolved over 5,000 years and is based on empirical observation coupled with the wisdom of the healers.

“We have several patients like Mrs Shekhar who were immobilized with pain when they came in, and leave driving two-wheelers,” Gangadharan says.

“People need to have patience, faith (in Ayurveda) and the willingness to drink bitter-tasting medicines.”

He adds a caveat, stressing that for Ayurveda to work, “it is important to find the right Ayurvedic physician. The right Ayurvedic physician will prescribe a combination of dietary, lifestyle modifications, purifying treatments and medicine, and it is best to go to a physician recommended by friends and family. It is harder to find a good Ayurvedic physician than it is to find a good allopathic physician.”

Bhushan Patwardhan, director, Interdisciplinary School of Health Sciences, Pune, says: “While there are 600,000 practising Ayurvedic physicians in India today, it is difficult to find a physician who combines the practice of pure Ayurvedic tradition with an understanding of modern medicine.”

He adds: “There are certain Ayurvedic traditions, like the Kerala tradition, where much of the knowledge is passed down by word of mouth, from one generation to the next. In such a scenario, it is prudent to go to a physician who is a third or fourth generation practising Ayurvedic physician from a family of physicians.”

Since Ayurvedic practice does not have a rigorous regulatory system, it is often difficult to assess the quality of the physician. Prasanna Paranjape, an Ayurvedic physician from Pune, says he finds that doctors at many of the Ayurvedic spa clinics which have mushroomed in the city aren’t aware of the right methods of administering Ayurvedic therapies.

Ayurveda, of course, does not have the solutions to all medical problems and an Ayurvedic physician should know when to turn to Western medicine.

“For surgical procedures, or in cases of severe infection, and for the acute treatment of diabetes, it is best to turn to Western medicine,” says Paranjape. “Ayurvedic medicines aren’t standardized and don’t go through the rigorous clinical trials that Western medical drugs are put through, but that will change in the next couple of years with new US food and drug administration (FDA) guidelines that are being considered for Ayurvedic medicines.”

Till the FDA guidelines are imposed, there is research under way proving the usefulness of certain Ayurvedic medicines in Western medical practice. A recent study by Arvind Chopra and colleagues from the Symbiosis School of Biomedical Sciences, Pune, showed that an Ayurvedic formulation containing the herb guduchi is as effective in treating osteoarthritis-related knee pain as the allopathic alternative, a combination of glucosamine and celecoxib. The study results were published in Rheumatology in January.

Curcumin, the main constituent of haldi (turmeric), a household Indian spice, has been researched in more than 4,000 published clinical studies. And these studies found curcumin has anti-cancer, anti-inflammatory and anti-oxidative properties.

To Ayurvedic physicians this comes as no surprise. Haldi has been prescribed by Ayurveda since ancient times for the very same properties. Haldi also has other substances like turmerone and polysaccharides that make it more potent than curcumin alone. In fact, in a review of the medical literature on the role of haldi in the treatment of cancer by Ashok B. Vaidya and colleagues from the Kasturba Health Society, Mumbai, the reviewers found that haldi unequivocally reduces the pain and complications of cancer chemotherapy and helps to prevent metastasis, or the spread of cancer through the body. This led the authors to conclude that haldi should be used as a regular supplement in cancer therapy. The review was published in the Clinical Cancer Investigation Journal in 2012.

Research studies like these cast Ayurveda in a favourable light. It can still be relevant in managing our health as long as we seek it from a reputed Ayurvedic physician while keeping its limitations in mind.

Sujata Kelkar Shetty, PhD, writes on public health issues and is a research scientist trained at the National Institutes of Health in Bethesda, US.
