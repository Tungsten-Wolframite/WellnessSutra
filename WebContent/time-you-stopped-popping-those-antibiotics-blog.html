Time you stopped popping those antibiotics


Antibiotics will only be available on prescription in India
Published in Mint-Leading Business Newspaper
Mon, Feb 17 2014. 06 18 PM

Self-prescription and overuse of antibiotics can lead to drug resistance—the medicines won’t work when you actually have a bacterial infection

Antibiotics are used liberally during this season of coughs, colds and sore throats, even though most sinus, ear infections and sore throats are mostly viral infections. Antibiotics, as the name suggests, should only be used to treat bacterial diseases, and using it for anything else can lead to antibiotic resistance, which means the medicines won’t work when you actually have a bacterial disease.

This in turn can have serious consequences with mankind returning to a pre-antibiotic era where bacterial infections were untreatable. Simple bacterial diseases would turn fatal, and epidemics of diseases like pneumonia and tuberculosis could become a reality.

Antibiotic resistance is one of the world’s most critical public health threats, according to the US’ Centers for Disease Control And Prevention and the World Health Organization. It has emerged as a very serious health issue in India, according to the Chennai Declaration, an association of medical societies across India specially created in 2012 to formulate a road map to tackle the menace of antibiotic resistance. One of the reasons antibiotics are overused in India is easy accessibility—you don’t need always need a prescription to purchase them. The efforts of the Chennai Declaration has led to recent regulation that says that from 1 March, antibiotics can no longer be sold over the counter. This is a step forward in the right direction.

“I often get patients who have already self-prescribed a course of antibiotics before showing up at my clinic,” says Vivek Nangia, director, pulmonology and infectious diseases, Fortis Ft Lt Rajan Dhall Hospital, Vasant Kunj, Delhi. “You really shouldn’t be using antibiotics unless a doctor prescribes it, as it can lead to antibiotic resistance.”

The other problem with taking antibiotics without prescription is that certain antibiotics can interact with drugs that you are already taking or can cause complications if you have a pre-existing chronic condition like diabetes, according to Ravindra Gupta, consultant, internal medicine, Columbia Asia hospital, Gurgaon. Antibiotics can also have side effects like headache, yeast infections, abdominal pain and diarrhoea. Some people take longer to recover from the side effects of antibiotics than from the original illness itself.

Doctors say there is pressure to prescribe antibiotics because the patient asks for it, thinking it will get them back to normal life faster. It is far easier and faster for a doctor to prescribe an antibiotic when a patient is asking for it then to educate the patient. Sometimes a genuine misdiagnosis of a viral infection for bacterial occurs. Often doctors misdiagnose on the basis of sputum colour, thinking that a darker sputum is caused by a bacterial infection. In reality, both bacterial and viral infections can produce dark, greenish sputum. As a patient, question your doctor by asking him or her if it’s a viral infection or a bacterial infection for which an antibiotic is prescribed. A bacterial infection in itself also doesn’t necessarily require antibiotics, since most are self-limiting, which means the infection will resolve on its own without medicine within a specific time-period. For example, though most cases of otitis media or ear infections are bacterial, 80% of them resolve on their own (seven-10 days on an average), according to a medical article by Kristina D. Mancini from the Avenel-Iselin Medical Group, New Jersey, US, and Ellen D. Mandel from Seton Hall University, New Jersey, titled “The SNAP treatment approach for children with otitis media”, published in the Journal of the American Academy of Physician Assistants in February 2012.
Assuming that the diagnosis is performed correctly and you do indeed need antibiotics, patient compliance, or the “patient taking the medicine at the appropriate dose, for the right period of time, is a huge issue”, says Dr Nangia. Once you start feeling better, you cannot stop taking the antibiotic. You need to take the antibiotic for as long and as often as it is prescribed.

“I have found in my practice that if an antibiotic is prescribed 6 hourly, often my patient will take it only twice a day instead of four times,” says Dr Nangia. Not taking the antibiotic for as long as it’s prescribed also leads to antibiotic resistance as all the bacteria don’t get killed and the ones that remain mutate and learn to survive in the presence of the antibiotic.

“Diarrhoea is a common side effect of taking antibiotics and 25% of the patients complaining of diarrhoea in my clinic are there because of antibiotic use,” says Manish Kak, consultant, gastroenterology, Columbia Asia hospital, Ghaziabad. This happens because antibiotics kill bacteria indiscriminately; the illness-causing bacteria in our bodies and the “good” bacteria residing in our intestines are both destroyed. We have 10 times as many bacteria in our intestines as we have cells in our bodies and they play a crucial role in digestion and in protecting our intestines from infectious bacteria.

So if you do indeed need to take antibiotics, take some probiotics to prevent antibiotic-related diarrhoea. Probiotics are supplements of “good” bacteria that can repopulate your intestines so that your intestines don’t lose their digestive and immune capacity while your body fights the “bad” bacteria.
